Franklin Gothic vs. Trade Gothic

History of Each Typeface

Franklin Gothic

Franklin Gothic, a typeface instantly recognizable for its bold lines and industrial feel, holds a significant place in American design history. Born in the early 20th century, it emerged from the hands of Morris Fuller Benton, a prolific typeface designer who served as the head of the American Type Founders (ATF) company. Benton, stationed in Jersey City, New Jersey, created Franklin Gothic in 1902, though it wasn't released as a commercial font until 1905. The specific purpose behind Franklin Gothic's design remains somewhat open to interpretation. However, the historical context offers valuable clues. The early 20th century witnessed a surge in industrialization and modernization across the United States. This period, often referred to as the Gilded Age, saw a growing demand for bold and impactful visuals in advertising and branding.

Franklin Gothic, with its robust letterforms and lack of serifs (decorative flourishes), perfectly captured this industrial spirit. It exuded a sense of strength, reliability, and directness, qualities highly sought after by businesses of the era. Some historians speculate that Benton might have drawn inspiration from the emerging "grotesque" sans-serif typefaces gaining popularity in Europe at the time. However, Franklin Gothic stood out with its bolder weight and a more condensed design, making it a uniquely American interpretation of the trend. Franklin Gothic's success was immediate. Its bold presence made it ideal for headlines, posters, and packaging. Interestingly, the name itself, a homage to Benjamin Franklin, further solidified the font's connection to American identity. Franklin, a polymath known for his role in the American Revolution and his work as a printer, embodied the ideals of innovation and practicality that the typeface seemed to represent.

Today, over a century after its creation, Franklin Gothic remains a popular choice for designers. Its versatility has extended beyond its industrial roots, finding use in everything from modern logos and website design to book covers and editorial layouts. Franklin Gothic's enduring appeal lies in its ability to convey strength, clarity, and a timeless American spirit.

Trade Gothic

Trade Gothic, a typeface recognized for its clean lines, geometric forms, and bold presence, boasts a rich history intertwined with the vibrant Art Deco movement. Jackson Burke, a prominent American typeface designer working at the American Type Foundry (ATF) in New York City, is credited with its creation in the 1920s. The bustling metropolis of New York during this period was a hotbed of artistic innovation. Art Deco, characterized by its geometric shapes, bold lines, and a sense of modernity, permeated various aspects of design. Burke, aiming to capture this artistic spirit, meticulously crafted Trade Gothic.

Trade Gothic's design choices reflected the Art Deco aesthetic. It belonged to the sans-serif family, lacking the decorative flourishes of traditional fonts. Instead, it embraced geometric shapes, with letterforms constructed from circles, squares, and rectangles. This clean and modern look resonated with the Art Deco movement, making Trade Gothic a popular choice for advertising, packaging, and branding materials during the 1920s and 1930s.Trade Gothic's popularity stemmed not only from its visual alignment with Art Deco but also from its functionality. The typeface offered a condensed form, allowing designers to fit more information into a smaller space. This proved valuable for headlines, titles, and layouts with limited space constraints.

While its initial purpose was likely to complement the Art Deco movement, Trade Gothic's design transcended its era. Its clean lines, strong presence, and improved readability ensured its continued relevance. In 2009, the Font Bureau design team created Trade Gothic Next, a refined version that preserved the core characteristics while offering additional weights and italic styles, further extending its versatility for contemporary design needs. Today, Trade Gothic remains a popular choice for designers worldwide. Its ability to convey a sense of authority, modernity, and professionalism makes it suitable for a variety of applications, from corporate logos and marketing materials to website design and editorial layouts. Trade Gothic's legacy endures as a timeless and versatile font, carrying the spirit of Art Deco design into the digital age.
